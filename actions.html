06 — Concrete Steps

Not more words.
Actions, starting now.

Everything above is analysis. Analysis without action is just more words — which is already one of the core problems in this relationship. Here are specific, immediate, and longer-term steps that could genuinely change the dynamic.

This Month

Replace Apology With Commitment

After the next conflict — and there will be one — try this: instead of "I'm sorry, I love you, please forgive me," say: "I'm sorry. Here is the one specific thing I will do differently." Then do it for two weeks. Not forever. Just two weeks. Show that the word and the action can match, even briefly.

Esita especially
This Month

The Weekly Check-In Call

Set one time per week — say, Sunday evening — that is the "how are we actually doing" call. Not romantic, not sexy, not a fight. Just: how was your week, what do you need from me this week, is there anything bothering you that we should talk about? Regular maintenance prevents emergency repairs.

Both
Ongoing

Retire Three Phrases Permanently

"You're using me" — replace with "I feel invisible." "Goodbye forever" — replace with "I need space right now, I'll come back to this tomorrow." "Choose: me or your mother" — replace with "When we're together, I need to be your first priority for that time." Three phrase swaps, enormous impact.

Aditya especially
Ongoing

Celebrate What Works

The website. The Valentine's weekend. The midnight Karan Singh conversations. The morning love messages. These are real and they happen regularly. Both have got so accustomed to fighting that they sometimes forget to name what's good. Once a week — just one moment — acknowledge something specific: "I noticed you did X this week. It mattered."

Both
Immediate · This Week

Assume Positive Intent

When a text feels sharp, distant, or delayed, pause before answering. Before reacting with anger or hurt, actively ask: "Is there a version of this where they aren't trying to hurt me?" Much of the conflict begins by immediately assuming the worst possible motive.

Both
This Month

Redefine "Quality Time"

Because of the long distance, being "together" often devolves into just leaving the phone on speaker while doing other things. Pick 30 minutes a week of uninterrupted, screen-to-screen presence where absolute focus is given to each other. Genuine presence solves what hours of distraction cannot.

07 — In Closing

What remains
when the dust settles.

Reading 90 days of conversation between two people, you see everything — the tenderness and the cruelty, the effort and the avoidance, the love that keeps returning even after every goodbye. What's remarkable about Aditya and Esita is not the fighting. Couples fight. What's remarkable is that after every explosion, they come back. Every single time.

That's not nothing. That's actually something quite rare.

The fights are loud because the love is loud. The pain is acute because the investment is deep. Aditya doesn't spiral at 1am over someone he's indifferent about. Esita doesn't beg and weep and keep coming back for someone she's merely using. These are two people who are genuinely, stubbornly, perhaps inconveniently in love with each other — and who have not yet found a way to fight that doesn't also hurt what they're fighting for.

That is the only thing that needs to change. Not the love. Not the commitment. Not the future they're planning (engagement on her birthday, family conversations, marriage someday). Just the way they argue — the tools they reach for when the old wound gets touched again.

The question isn't whether they love each other. That's obvious, and settled. The question is whether they love each other enough to change how they fight. Because the love, on its own, isn't the problem. It never was.

Radhe Radhe.
