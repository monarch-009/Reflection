05 — What Can Change

Honest suggestions,
for both of them.

For Aditya

01

Retire the "goodbye forever" threat

Evidence: 6+ times in 60 days

You have said goodbye forever, threatened to block, and ended things at least six times in this data alone. She has learned not to take it seriously — which means when you genuinely need her to understand the gravity of your pain, the words carry no weight. Either act on them or stop using them. The threat without the action is the worst of both worlds.

02

No serious conversations after 10pm

Evidence: Almost every explosion happened 10pm–2am

Your most articulate, most honest, and most damaging messages all come late at night. Your 1:39am message on March 27 was accurate — but you wrote it when you were exhausted, traveling, and emotionally raw. The words were right; the timing ensured she received them as an attack, not as truth. Write it during the day, or wait until morning.

03

Replace "you're using me" with what you actually mean

Evidence: Used as the ultimate weapon, repeatedly

What you actually mean is: "I feel invisible. I invested everything and it doesn't seem to matter." That is true and it deserves to be heard. But "you're using me" is an accusation that can never be resolved — it shuts down conversation rather than opening it. She cannot defend against a verdict. She can respond to a need.

04

Integrate your two selves

Evidence: Karan Singh vs adityasinghmoni — same person, different registers

The tenderness and playfulness of the Karan Singh conversations and the warmth of the morning messages are as much you as the wounded, ultimatum-giving Aditya. The second number gives you a "fresh start" space — but it also means you're compartmentalizing rather than integrating. She deserves to have all of you in the same relationship.

05

Be specific about what visits require

Evidence: Multiple visits ended in conflict over unmet unspoken expectations

Before you travel, say clearly: "I need us to have one full day together, no calls from work, no family obligations, just us." Give her the chance to either commit to that or to say it's not possible yet — either answer is more useful than arriving with silent expectations that get broken.

06

Stop asking her to choose between you and her mother

Evidence: This ultimatum appears repeatedly and never works

You will never win that fight, and more importantly, winning it would damage her — which would ultimately damage the relationship. What you actually want is a protected space in her life, not the destruction of her family bond. Ask for the specific thing you need: "When I'm with you, I need to be your first priority for that time." That's achievable. "Leave your mother" is not.

07

Ask how she is — genuinely, when things are hard

Evidence: You noted yourself that she never asked what you were going through

You are right that she doesn't always ask about your pain during her own difficulties. But reviewing the chats — you also rarely ask about hers during yours. She started a new job, moved cities, had health issues, was managing family pressure. There are moments where a simple "how are you actually doing today" could have changed the direction of the whole conversation.

For Esita

01

Let your apologies become commitments, not endings

Evidence: Same apology, same incident, dozens of times

Your apologies are genuine. He knows this, even when he says otherwise. The problem is that they've become a way to end the discomfort of conflict rather than actually address what caused it. Try this: after the apology, name one specific thing you'll do differently. "I'm sorry. And from now, when you're here, my phone goes on silent for work calls during our time." One concrete action is worth more than a hundred sorrys.

02

Stop making promises in conflict that you can't keep in reality

Evidence: "I won't make friends," "mummy won't interfere," repeated

When you're scared of losing him, you promise things that aren't realistic — "I won't talk to colleagues outside work," "mummy won't come between us." These promises, made under emotional pressure, make you appear less trustworthy each time they break — even though the break comes from life's natural complexity. It's better to say less, and do more, than to over-promise and under-deliver.

03

When he visits — protect that time fiercely

Evidence: Multiple visits disrupted by avoidable interruptions

He travels hundreds of kilometers to spend time with you. Once — just once — being 40 minutes late or taking a call during that time feels to him like confirmation of everything he fears. This doesn't mean your family or job don't matter. It means: when the visit is happening, treat it as the sacred thing it is to him. Cut calls. Close apps. Be present. Even one visit where he feels completely chosen would change the dynamic meaningfully.

04

Examine the begging-for-affection pattern honestly

Evidence: Kiss requests mid-serious conflict, repeatedly

Right in the middle of serious conversations — about trust, about feeling used, about fundamental relationship questions — you pivot to asking for kisses or hugs. Sometimes this is genuine love spilling through. Sometimes it's conflict avoidance. He experiences it as: "she wants the comfort of me without doing the work of us." Even if that's not your intention, it's worth being aware of.

05

Build the habit of informing, not just updating

Evidence: The home trip — decided first, told second, framed as asking

The home trip incident hurt him not because you went home — going home was completely reasonable — but because you had decided before you asked, and he felt the "asking" was performance rather than genuine consultation. He doesn't need permission for your decisions. He needs to feel like his perspective was genuinely considered before you decided. There's a difference between "I'm thinking of going home, what do you think?" and "I'm going home" — and he feels that difference acutely.

06

Work on your limits with your mother — for yourself

Evidence: Mummy has entered the relationship space multiple times

Not because he asks you to. Not because it's wrong to love your mother. But because you, reading these chats, can probably see times when her needs or preferences shaped your actions in this relationship without you consciously choosing that. You are 22/23, working, independent, building your own life. Part of that life is deciding — clearly, deliberately — what space belongs to you and your partner that isn't automatically overridden by family expectations.

07

Ask him how he is — when he's the one in pain

Evidence: He noted this directly on March 22

He wrote: "From yesterday you have asked once how I am. From what I'm going through." He's right. When you're both in conflict, you're often defending yourself, explaining yourself, or trying to fix his mood — but not asking what's underneath his anger. Sometimes the fight would de-escalate entirely if, instead of a counter-argument, you said: "Tell me what's actually going on with you right now."

"Real change doesn't happen in the apology. It happens in what comes after."

— Honest suggestions for two people worth fighting for.
